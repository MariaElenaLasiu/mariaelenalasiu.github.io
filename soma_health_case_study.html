01 — Problem

The wrong product — and the research that killed it

SOMA Health started as a physician-facing organ-centric data visualization tool for Italian hospitals. After consulting with a healthcare data specialist and a privacy lawyer, the verdict was clear: regulatory barriers (GDPR, FSE integration, hospital procurement cycles) made that version unfeasible without institutional partnerships we couldn't yet secure. Rather than push forward blindly, I used research to redirect.

❌ What we killed — and why

The physician dashboard required hospital-level data access, which triggers GDPR Article 9 (sensitive health data), Italian privacy law, and a procurement timeline of 12–24 months. Research conversations with a privacy lawyer and a healthcare integration specialist confirmed these weren't solvable constraints at early-stage. The honest call was to pivot before spending development budget.

🔄 The insight that reframed everything

The data chaos problem doesn't live inside hospitals — it lives in the hands of the 8 million caregiver managing their elderly relatives' healthcare from WhatsApp, their camera roll, and piles of paper.

📱

The scattered documents problem

Medical reports scattered across WhatsApp threads, email chains, camera rolls, and physical paper — impossible to find in an emergency, impossible to share coherently with a new specialist.

👩‍💼

8M caregivers, mostly invisible

~8 million Italians — predominantly women aged 45–60 who also work — manage heavy administrative healthcare loads for elderly relatives with no tools built for them.

🏥

Clinical risk, not just inconvenience

Missing documents at a specialist visit or ER visit don't just cause stress — they cause delays, repeat exams, and clinical decisions made without full information.

🔒

Consumer-grade data, not hospital data

The new model processes documents caregivers already own — photos of reports — sidestepping hospital-level GDPR barriers entirely. Feasible from day one.

02 — The Product Concept

SOMA Health: photo → organised → shared in 30 seconds

The app enables caregivers to photograph medical documents after each appointment. AI automatically organises them by specialisation. Documents are shared securely with doctors via a time-limited unique code. No hospital integration required. No GDPR Article 9 exposure. Built around a behaviour caregivers already have — taking photos — not one they need to learn.

⚠️ But this is still a hypothesis

We believe this solves a real, high-priority problem for Italian caregivers. We do not yet have evidence that it does. That is exactly what the research programme is designed to establish — before a line of production code is written.

03 — Research Process

A 3-phase lean programme to de-risk the concept

Each phase has a precise question to answer, a deliberate method choice, and go/no-go criteria that determine whether we proceed. Nothing is exploratory for the sake of it.

The 5 hypotheses this programme will prove or kill

H1 · Fragmentation
Documents are managed chaotically
Most caregivers use WhatsApp, camera roll, and email — no centralised system.
H2 · Frequency
≥60% fail to find a document when needed
At least 60% have experienced retrieval failure in the past year (visit, ER, new specialist).
H3 · Time cost
≥1–2 hours/month on admin
Caregivers spend significant monthly time on healthcare administration for their relative.
H4 · Clinical impact
≥20% report real-world consequences
Delays, duplicate exams, or clinical risk caused by missing documents at the point of care.
H5 · Solution fit
"Photo after visit" feels like relief, not burden
The concept is perceived as a natural improvement on current behaviour — not another tool to manage.

Phase 1
Decision

Why interviews before the prototype — and why "show me" over "tell me"

Showing the concept first anchors users on our solution rather than their actual behaviour. I structured Phase 1 to explore current reality for 40 minutes before any concept mention. The most powerful technique: asking caregivers to show their current document system — WhatsApp chaos, blurry photo folders, cardboard folders — which generates richer data than any self-report question.

Phase 1
Action

Interview structure designed around 4 moments

Warm-up → Current behaviour → Pain points → Indirect concept validation. The indirect validation block uses imagination ("if such a tool existed...") rather than evaluation — critical at Phase 1, where we want to understand mental models before testing a specific design.

Phase 2
Decision

Van Westendorp over direct pricing — again

Same reasoning as SugarWise: direct price questions anchor respondents and are especially unreliable when users default to expecting free tools. PSM extracts four psychological thresholds — too cheap, acceptable, expensive, too expensive — without anchoring bias. Particularly important with caregivers, who may resist monetising healthcare tasks for loved ones.

Phase 3
Action

Concept test before prototype — comprehension before usability

H5 is about fit in routine, not task completion. I'll test whether caregivers understand and desire the concept before testing whether they can operate a prototype — separating desirability questions from usability questions, which are often conflated in early-stage testing.

Phase 1 interview guide

🔥 Warm-up — Contextualise, don't collect

5 min
Goal: establish rapport and understand the caregiving context before touching any specific topic.
Raccontami di te: chi hai in cura, da quanto tempo, come è organizzata la tua settimana rispetto a questa responsabilità.

📋 Current behaviour — The "show me" moment

15 min
⚡ "Mostrami" is the most powerful question in the UX toolkit. People show you the WhatsApp chaos, blurry photo folders, cardboard folders — more revealing than any self-report.
Descrivimi l'ultima volta che hai accompagnato [persona] da un medico. Cosa hai fatto prima, durante e dopo?
Dove tieni i documenti medici di [persona] in questo momento? Mostrami se puoi.
Raccontami un momento in cui hai avuto bisogno di un documento e non riuscivi a trovarlo.

😤 Pain points — Severity & impact

15 min
Goal: understand severity, emotional weight, and real-world consequences — not just inconvenience.
Qual è la cosa più frustrante nella gestione della salute di [persona]?
C'è mai stato un momento in cui non avere le informazioni giuste in tempo ha causato un problema reale?
Se potessi cambiare una sola cosa in questo processo, quale sarebbe?

💡 Indirect concept validation — Imagine, don't evaluate

10 min
Do not show the app. Use imagination to surface mental models and workarounds without anchoring on our specific design.
Se esistesse uno strumento che ti permettesse di avere tutto in un posto e condividerlo in 30 secondi col medico, dove si inserirebbe nella tua routine?
In che modo potrebbe facilitare la condivisione delle informazioni con altri membri della famiglia?

👥 Recruitment criteria & channels

Channels: Facebook groups and online communities for caregivers of elderly people; personal networks and referrals.
Inclusion: Manages healthcare of an elderly relative ≥1x/month (bookings, visits, exams, documents) · Aged 40–65.
Exclusion: Primary caregivers of people with severe disability (Legge 104 – autism, severe disability) — different needs and complexity requiring separate research.

05 — Impact So Far

What research has already changed

🔄

Killed a non-viable product

Regulatory and technical analysis — before any development — confirmed the physician dashboard was unfeasible at early stage. Research saved months of misdirected effort.

🎯

Identified a larger, accessible market

8 million Italian caregivers vs. a subset of Italian hospital physicians. B2C via App Store vs. B2B via hospital procurement. Faster path to revenue, lower regulatory barrier.

🧪

Designed a defensible research programme

5 testable hypotheses, 3 phases, go/no-go criteria. The programme is documented for Sardegna Ricerche funding requirements and investor due diligence.

📄

Ethics infrastructure in place

Informed consent documentation completed and ready for participant recruitment. Inclusion/exclusion criteria defined to protect vulnerable populations.
