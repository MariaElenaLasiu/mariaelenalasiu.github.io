01 — Problem

Founders had a prototype but no answers

SugarWise was built by a mother-and-aunt team who had lived paediatric T1D caregiving firsthand. They had conviction — but knew that without research, they were making expensive product bets in the dark.

❌

Are there fundamental needs left unmet?

Founders developed the product with a specific scenario in mind: parents returning to familiar restaurants who couldn't recall previous insulin doses for the same meal — and needed a simple way to log and retrieve that information quickly. Was that their real need?

❌

Which features justify payment?

The team had 10+ features in scope. No evidence about which ones users valued enough to pay for.

❌

What is the viable price point?

Charging for a health app in Italy requires navigating deep cultural resistance to paid healthcare tools.

❌

Are we targeting the right users?

Pump users and pen users were treated as one audience. Was that assumption valid?

⚠️ The risk

Building the wrong product and wasting 6+ months of engineering time without validating that the product actually solved a real problem in a way users would pay for.

🎯 Mission

Validate product-market fit and define the go-to-market strategy before a single line of production code was written.

02 — Research Process

How I ran a 3-phase programme to de-risk the product

Each phase had a specific question to answer, a deliberate method choice, and a concrete output that fed the next phase. Nothing was arbitrary.

Phase 1
Problem

The problem I was solving for

The founding team built around a specific moment: a parent back at the same restaurant, ordering the same pizza, with no memory of how much insulin they'd given their child last time. The assumption was that logging and recalling meal-specific doses was the core struggle. Before building further, I needed to pressure-test that against real caregiving behaviour — and find out whether that scenario was truly where families felt the most friction.

Phase 1
Decision

The method I chose — and why

I designed two-part moderated sessions: 30 minutes of lived experience exploration before any prototype interaction. I made this decision deliberately — showing the prototype first would have anchored users on the product rather than revealing their real mental models. I recruited through regional Facebook diabetes groups after finding professional networks too slow and thin.

Phase 1
Action

What I did

I conducted 6 in-depth interviews via Zoom with parents of children aged 4–10. Remote sessions were a deliberate choice — they let me observe the home caregiving environment in real time. I ran thematic analysis across all sessions and compiled a prioritised friction matrix of 8 critical issues for the design team.

Phase 1
Impact

What changed as a result

The core product narrative was reframed. The problem was not the maths on carbs — it was the constant cognitive weight of deciding, delegating, and managing uncertainty across every context of family life. This reframe changed the product's value proposition entirely: from calculator to cognitive relief partner.

"Our whole day revolves around insulin and meals... we try to live like before, even if it's not ideal." — Mother of 8-year-old

Phase 2
Problem

The problem I was solving for

Phase 1 produced 8 friction points and a redesign. But redesigning without re-testing is just guessing. I needed to prove that the changes actually resolved the original problems — and hadn't created new ones.

Phase 2
Decision

The method I chose — and why

I designed a comparative prototype test specifically targeting the 8 friction points from Phase 1. Rather than running an open exploration, I structured tasks to generate direct before/after evidence. We also decided at this point not to test the pen user flow — it was unready, and validating an unfinished design would have produced misleading data. I scoped it as a Phase 4 recommendation instead.

Phase 2
Action

What I did

I ran 5 targeted follow-up interviews with new participants, using the same Facebook group recruitment channel validated in Phase 1. Each session tested specific redesigned flows with a think-aloud protocol, generating direct comparative evidence on each intervention.

Phase 2
Impact

What changed as a result

6 of 8 friction points were validated as resolved. The glass-box AI transparency feature generated the single most positive reaction of the entire programme. The "relevant factors" module (stress, activity, illness) was called out as something users had never seen in any other app — validating a major differentiator.

"This relevant factors section is something we've never found in any app — it would be a huge help." — Father of 12-year-old

Phase 3
Problem

The problem I was solving for

The product was validated qualitatively. But to pitch investors and build a sustainable business, the team needed quantified demand, a defensible price point, and an institutional endorsement. Qualitative data alone couldn't provide that.

Phase 3
Decision

The method I chose — and why

I chose the Van Westendorp Price Sensitivity Meter over standard price questions because PSM extracts four psychological thresholds without anchoring respondents to a number — critical in a context where users default to expecting healthcare tools to be free. I simultaneously commissioned a structured expert interview with the President of local pediatric diabetes association to stress-test the business model from the institutional side.

Phase 3

What I did

I designed and deployed a 20-question online survey (n=54) via local association's Facebook network — a partnership established by leveraging the community trust built in earlier phases. I screened data for internal inconsistencies, excluding 6 responses with logical contradictions before analysis, and ran full segmentation by therapy type (pump vs pen).

Phase 3
Impact

What changed as a result

The go-to-market strategy was pivoted. Pen users showed 3× higher willingness to pay and an 84% premium conversion rate — versus 71% for pump users. This single finding changed who the product should target first, directly reducing CAC and increasing launch conversion probability. Additionally, the expert interview surfaced an entirely new B2B revenue stream: restaurant nutritional certification, which became the third pillar of the business model.

03 — Key Findings

What the data said — and what it changed

🧠

The mental load IS the disease

The core struggle was not carb calculation accuracy — it was the constant cognitive weight of deciding, delegating, and managing uncertainty 24/7. This reframed the entire product value proposition.

"Our whole day revolves around insulin and meals." — Mother, 8-year-old

📐

42 variables, not just carbs

Over 40 factors affect blood glucose: stress, hormones, exercise, food order, illness. A linear carb calculator was always going to fail. This drove the adaptive algorithm redesign.

🔍

Transparency IS the feature

AI distrust was high — parents needed to stay in control of their child's dosing. Showing calculation logic ("glass box") measurably increased trust and acceptance rates.

🎉

The need to say "yes"

Families wanted social normalcy — birthday cakes, restaurants, school lunches — not just glucose control. The app needed to enable confidence in social contexts, not just at the kitchen table.

💡 The segmentation insight that changed everything

Pen users showed 17% higher willingness to pay and an 84% premium conversion rate vs 71% for pump users. Pump users already have automated dosing built into hardware. Pen users manage calculations manually — the app fills a genuine gap. This finding pivoted the go-to-market from broad to precision targeting.

05 — Prototype Evolution

8 friction points. 8 targeted fixes. Re-tested.

Each finding from Phase 1 became a specific design brief. Each fix was tested in Phase 2. Here's the before/after evidence.

🔴 Before

Users couldn't tell if macro values referred to a 100g standard or their estimated portion — undermining trust in every calculation.

🟢 After

Dual-column display showing both values simultaneously, plus explicit portion weight. Validated as "resolved" in Phase 2. ✅

🔴 Before

Black-box AI suggestions felt unsafe when the stakes were a child's insulin dose. Parents felt they couldn't verify or trust the output.

🟢 After

"Glass box" calculation view shows every variable and logic step. Users described feeling "supported but always in control." ✅

🔴 Before

App only handled carbohydrates despite 40+ variables affecting blood glucose. The formula felt incomplete and frustrating in complex real-world situations.

🟢 After

"Relevant factors" module added within the meal-logging flow. Phase 2: "We've never seen this in any app — it would be a huge help." ✅

🔴 Before

Users switched to Yuka for packaged food scanning then returned to the app — a daily friction point that fragmented the experience.

🟢 After

Native barcode scanning integrated. "Everything in the same place" cited as significant quality-of-life improvement. ✅

🔴 Before

Interface designed around pump logic. Pen users think in "units" — not complex bolus parameters. Terms like "extended bolus" created confusion and distrust.

🟢 Recommendation

Dedicated pen flow prototyped but deliberately not tested within this programme. Scoped as Phase 4 priority — highest-value segment, highest unmet need. 🗓

06 — Research Impact

What the research actually changed

Research has value only when it changes decisions. Every finding had a downstream consequence.

🎯

Go-to-market pivot

Segmentation data identified pen users as 3× more valuable early adopters. Research recommended pen-first, backed by quantified WTP data — directly reducing CAC and increasing launch conversion probability.

💰

Defensible pricing model

Van Westendorp produced a validated price range of €4.50–€10.00/month. Expert interview surfaced a one-time €20 access fee as culturally superior to monthly billing — a finding not in the original research plan.

🏗️

MVP feature hierarchy

Photo estimation (61% WTP) validated as the hero premium feature — not the bolus calculator. This directly informed sprint prioritisation and engineering resource allocation.

🔬

Algorithm reframed

Finding that 40+ variables mattered led to a fundamental product pivot: from carb calculator to adaptive decision-support system. This changed the core value proposition and technical architecture.

📋

Prioritised research roadmap

Delivered a Phase 4 recommendation: dedicated pen user prototype validation with defined KPIs and recruitment channels — ensuring continuity and no loss of institutional knowledge.

07 — Ethics & Operational Rigour

The work behind the work

Researching families around a chronic childhood illness requires operational and ethical precision that goes well beyond standard UX practice.

🛡️

Participant protection

Sessions designed with explicit stop-points for emotional distress. Sessions were ready to be shortened when participants became upset discussing diagnosis trauma — data completeness was never prioritised over participant safety.

📋

Informed consent

All participants briefed on data use, audience, and right to withdraw. Consent confirmed at session start — not assumed from recruitment agreement.

🔒

Data integrity

6 survey responses excluded for internal logical contradictions before analysis. Documented transparently in the final report — no silent exclusions.

⚖️

Representation awareness

83% female participation flagged as reflecting real caregiving distribution (mothers primary in 72% of cases nationally) — not a recruitment failure. Context built into all analysis and recommendations.

🧭

Business alignment

Maintained independence between user advocacy and business viability. Where user expectations (free provision) conflicted with sustainability, both perspectives were presented clearly with evidence-based recommendations.

📌

Scope discipline

When pen user complexity emerged mid-project, I resisted testing an unfinished flow. Unvalidated changes produce unreliable data. Scoped clearly, documented the gap, made it a priority recommendation.

08 — Reflection

What I'd do differently — and what I'd protect

✅ The decision I'd protect

The two-part interview structure — empathy before prototype — was a judgment call I almost didn't make under time pressure. By sequencing empathy first, I surfaced the cognitive load insight that became the entire product narrative. If I'd led with the prototype, users would have told me what they thought of the app. Instead, they told me what their life was actually like.

🔄 What I'd do differently

I'd screen for therapy type (pen vs pump) in Phase 1 recruitment. The segmentation insight came in Phase 2 — too late to build a pen prototype within this programme. Given that pen users became the highest-value early adopter segment, earlier discovery would have allowed me to test a dedicated pen flow within scope rather than handing it off.

💡 The assumption that didn't survive users

The founding team believed the core anxiety was about getting the maths right. Users taught me the anxiety was about everything surrounding the maths — school, delegation, social situations, fear of failure at 2am. That reframe was the most important output of the entire programme. It changed not just the product, but how the startup communicates its value.
